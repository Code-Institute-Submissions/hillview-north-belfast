  • The DUP and the Housing Department

    Nigel Dodds MP and the DUP

    Nigel Dodds is the MP for North Belfast and Deputy Leader of the Democratic Unionist Party (DUP).

    The DUP electoral majority in north Belfast has shrunk significantly over the decades.

    In the 2015 General Election, Dodds had an almost 5,000 lead over his nearest rival (Sinn Fein). In 2017, this itself had dwindled to 2,000 lead.

    These party political electoral concerns have coincided with consecutive interventions by DUP politicians to intervene in social housing provision and prepetuate religious inequality.

    Since 1999, the DUP have held the housing Ministry seven times (14 years in total). Two of the Ministers (Nigel Dodds MP and Nelson McCausland) have had direect consitutnecy interests in north Belfast housing provision.

    Ministerial interventions in social housing have taken the form of denying religious inequality exists, preventing social housing being built in areas of chronic need (areas with Catholic majorities), and promoting new social housing in communities of little to no need for additional social housing (areas with predominately Protestant majorities).

  • Frank Boyd and Killultagh Estates

    Frank Boyd and Killultagh Estates
    Frank Boyd is Managing Director of Killultagh Estates, the company which owns the Hillview site.

    Killultagh Estates was provided with over £1.8m in rates exaemptions and relief for the Hillvew site between 2006 and 2017. For most of this period, as revealed by the BBC Spotlight documentary, there has been an illegal, for-proft Sunday Market operating on the premises.

    Killultagh Estates was also provided £>>> Financial Assistance by the housing Department in 2003/4, which was subsequently written off in 2008.

  • Belfast City Council and the Housing Executive

    Belfast City Council and the Housing Executive
    On 15th August 2017, Belfast City Council Planning Committee voted – by a margin of 7-6 – to allow an application to proceed for Hillview which did not include a single home to help addressing homelessness.

    There was widespread disgust and anger at the decision.

    Equality Can’t Wait campaigners pointed to serious flaws in the process. Belfast City Council Planning Department and select Councillors ignored their public duties and favoured the developer throughout the process.

    Furthermore the Housing Executive, in flagrant breach of their duties under the Good Friday Agreement, had effectively blocked social housing on the site by requiring unionist consent as prequsite.

    Planning applications for social housing cannot proceed without the support of the Housing Executive.
